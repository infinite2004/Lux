Figma Make Prompt — Luma V2

Design a polished, calming mobile app called Luma that focuses on eye comfort, visual wellness, and cumulative visual load. Luma is a soft, human-centered health app that helps people of different ages understand how light, glare, screen exposure, and contrast affect their eyes over time, then offers gentle tools to support comfort and recovery.

This app should feel like a modern visual wellness and eye-care companion, not an AI assistant, not a productivity app, and not a high-tech analytics dashboard. The design should feel warm, trustworthy, soft, human-designed, and health oriented. It should be suitable for a broad audience including teens, adults, older adults, and families, rather than being student-only.

The app is based on the concept that people often notice visual discomfort only after it has already built up. Luma helps users become more aware of their daily visual load by interpreting factors like brightness, glare, screen exposure, contrast, and time of day in a gentle and understandable way. The app is not a diagnostic medical tool. It is a wellness-focused companion for awareness, comfort, and healthier daily habits.

Use a color palette inspired by modern optometry and eye-care spaces:
	•	soft blues
	•	muted teals
	•	sage greens
	•	warm whites
	•	greige and light beige neutrals
	•	soft gray
	•	subtle navy accents
	•	small restrained touches of muted golden yellow or muted orange as accent only

Avoid bright red, harsh orange, aggressive warning colors, pure black backgrounds, or high-contrast glaring whites.

Use visual inspiration from optometrist offices and eye-care clinics:
	•	calming waiting-room softness
	•	gentle healthcare professionalism
	•	natural warmth
	•	rounded forms
	•	subtle shadows
	•	breathable spacing
	•	occasional natural material references like wood tones or plant-inspired warmth

The app should include soft visual representations of the eye, such as:
	•	iris-inspired rings
	•	eye comfort halos
	•	pupil-centered graphics
	•	visual comfort circles
	•	recovery arcs around an abstract eye shape

These eye motifs should feel elegant, symbolic, and modern, not clinical or anatomical.

The app should use soft, human health language such as:
	•	comfort
	•	ease
	•	visual load
	•	recovery
	•	sensitivity
	•	support
	•	calm
	•	gentle adjustment

Avoid language associated with productivity, optimization, or AI.

Create these 8 mobile screens for iPhone:

1. Onboarding

Create a short, elegant onboarding flow that introduces the app in a health-focused, human way. Do not use a long questionnaire. Keep onboarding brief, warm, and welcoming.

Use copy like:
	•	“Your eyes move through hundreds of light changes every day.”
	•	“Luma helps you notice visual strain before discomfort builds.”
	•	“Support your eyes with small, gentle adjustments.”

Include one simple preference step where the user can choose broad support goals such as:
	•	better comfort
	•	easier evenings
	•	less glare
	•	softer screen experience

Include a privacy screen with calm language:
	•	data stays private by default
	•	Luma supports awareness and comfort, not diagnosis
	•	sensing can be paused anytime
	•	manual control is always available

2. Today / Home Screen

Design a soft, welcoming home screen that feels like opening a health app.

Include:
	•	“Today” header
	•	a central eye-inspired visual, such as a soft iris ring or comfort halo, showing current eye comfort state
	•	current comfort label such as:
Comfortable
Building Strain
Sensitive
Recovering
	•	a gentle explanation such as:
“Your eyes have had a bright morning.”
“Glare and screen time are adding to your visual load.”
	•	primary CTA: Turn On Ease Mode
	•	small cards for:
today’s visual load
current environment
time since last reset

This screen should feel soft, clean, warm, and easy to trust.

3. Visual Load Timeline

Design a full-screen timeline showing how visual load changes throughout the day. Do not use a generic chart. Use a soft flowing ribbon, wave, or iris pulse that becomes thicker, brighter, or denser as visual load increases.

Show:
	•	morning, afternoon, evening, night
	•	moments of higher visual load
	•	calmer periods
	•	short recovery dips
	•	evening sensitivity buildup

Allow event markers like:
	•	prolonged screen use
	•	bright commute
	•	glare-heavy room
	•	quiet recovery period

When tapped, show a soft detail card with:
	•	what likely contributed
	•	how the eyes may have felt
	•	what may help next time

4. Eye Comfort Screen

Design a dedicated eye-focused health screen.

Include:
	•	large eye comfort ring or iris-inspired visualization
	•	current comfort level
	•	contributors to current visual load
	•	“what your eyes may need right now” section
	•	supportive suggestions such as:
“A softer screen may feel more comfortable.”
“Glare seems to be affecting your eyes more than brightness.”
“A short reset may help reduce sensitivity.”

This screen should strongly reinforce the app’s identity as a visual wellness app.

5. Your Environment

Design a calming environment interpretation screen, inspired by a room scan or environmental view.

Include:
	•	a room or surroundings scan preview
	•	labels such as:
bright but balanced
glare-heavy
visually quiet
high contrast
evening-bright
	•	soft overlay indicators for hotspots or uncomfortable areas
	•	plain-language recommendations such as:
“This space may feel easier with a lower screen angle.”
“Reflected light appears to be adding strain.”
“This room is bright but visually stable.”

This should feel like guidance from a care-oriented health app, not an engineering scanner.

6. Ease Mode

Design a soothing adjustment screen for reducing visual harshness.

Include controls for:
	•	lower brightness
	•	warmer tone
	•	reduced contrast
	•	reading comfort
	•	larger text
	•	quieter display mode

Use a gentle preview showing the screen becoming calmer and softer. The primary action should be Turn On Ease Mode.

This screen should clearly show that the app can not only monitor visual conditions but also influence them to support comfort.

7. Recovery

Design a recovery screen that feels like a short eye wellness reset.

Include:
	•	a supportive message such as:
“A short visual reset can help your eyes recover.”
	•	a look-away timer
	•	a calming progress ring
	•	a gentle animation or dimmed visual field
	•	a message showing comfort improving

This should feel restorative and practical, not like a meditation or productivity timer.

8. Patterns and Privacy

Create a combined or adjacent pair of screens if needed:
	•	one for Patterns
	•	one for Settings & Privacy

Patterns screen:
Include 3 or 4 soft insight cards such as:
	•	“Your eyes tend to feel better after short midday resets.”
	•	“Evening brightness may be increasing sensitivity.”
	•	“Glare affects your comfort more than brightness.”
	•	“Long reading sessions may benefit from more frequent easing.”

Settings & Privacy screen:
Include:
	•	pause sensing
	•	private mode
	•	adjust sensitivity
	•	delete today’s data
	•	not a diagnostic tool
	•	manual controls always available
	•	data is not shared with schools, employers, or third parties

UX and visual rules

The app should feel:
	•	calm
	•	health oriented
	•	human designed
	•	broad and inclusive across ages
	•	soft and restorative
	•	premium but approachable
	•	elegant and trustworthy

The app should not feel:
	•	like an AI assistant
	•	like a student productivity app
	•	like a quantified-self dashboard
	•	like a generic medical portal
	•	like a tech-heavy analytics tool

Use:
	•	soft gradients
	•	muted color palette
	•	eye-inspired health visuals
	•	rounded cards
	•	breathable spacing
	•	subtle iconography
	•	warm neutral backgrounds mixed with calming blues and greens

Avoid:
	•	loud red warnings
	•	harsh contrast
	•	cyberpunk UI
	•	excessive data density
	•	gamification
	•	sterile hospital design

The final result should feel like an award-worthy, human-centered eye wellness app that makes cumulative visual strain understandable and helps users care for their eyes through gentle awareness, environmental interpretation, and recovery support.
