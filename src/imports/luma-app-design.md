Figma Make Prompt — Luma V1

Design a polished, futuristic mobile app called Luma. Luma is a sensory wellness app that helps users understand and reduce their cumulative light burden across the day. The app is based on photoreception and the idea that people can perceive brightness in the moment but cannot meaningfully sense the accumulated visual strain caused by screens, glare, harsh lighting, contrast, and prolonged exposure over time.

This is not a medical diagnosis app, not a standard screen-time app, and not a generic productivity tool. It is a future-forward sensory interface that makes invisible visual burden visible and actionable. The app should feel calm, premium, emotionally intelligent, and highly intentional. Avoid health-dashboard clichés, aggressive alert colors, gamification, or overly technical/scientific UI. The interface should feel more like a light-awareness wellness system than a graph-heavy utility app.

The primary audience is students and young professionals ages 16 to 35 who spend long hours around laptops, phones, classrooms, offices, libraries, public transit, and bright digital signage. Their problem is that they only notice visual discomfort after it has already built up. Luma helps them perceive strain while it is building so they can adapt earlier.

The core concept is a speculative metric called the Light Burden Index, which combines factors like ambient light intensity, screen brightness, exposure duration, glare risk, time of day, contrast harshness, and optional comfort check-ins. Do not over-explain the science in the UI. Focus on how the app helps people understand and respond to their visual environment.

Use a dark mode first interface with soft luminous gradients, subtle glow, rounded corners, elegant typography, translucent cards, and minimal but expressive data visualizations. The app should visualize light burden as an atmospheric force, not just a number. Use a visual system based on:
	•	glowing ribbon timelines
	•	soft light clouds or orbs
	•	strain spikes as sharper brighter peaks
	•	recovery as cooling, dimming, smoothing
	•	clean, premium, minimal layouts

Use these 4 states only:
	•	Calm
	•	Building
	•	Strained
	•	Recovering

Do not use too many extra labels or metaphors. Keep the product focused.

Create these 8 mobile screens for iPhone:

1. Onboarding

Create a short, elegant onboarding flow introducing the idea of cumulative light burden. The emotional framing should be simple and human.

Include onboarding cards with copy like:
	•	“You can feel visual strain after it happens.”
	•	“Luma helps you notice it while it builds.”
	•	“Understand your light burden before discomfort takes over.”

Then include a simple setup flow asking:
	•	What usually bothers you most?
bright spaces, glare, screen fatigue, visual overstimulation, late-night phone use
	•	What do you want help with?
more comfort, less strain, better focus, easier nighttime wind-down
	•	What environments are most common for you?
school, office, home, transit, mixed

Include a privacy/permissions screen with calm copy:
	•	data stays private by default
	•	this app supports awareness and comfort, not diagnosis
	•	sensing can be paused anytime
	•	manual control is always available

The onboarding should feel premium, soft, modern, and visually distinct.

2. Home Screen

This is the main dashboard and should immediately tell the user how their current visual environment is affecting them.

Include:
	•	app name or top nav
	•	a large hero visualization, such as a glowing orb, cloud, or soft light field representing current light burden
	•	current state label: Calm, Building, Strained, or Recovering
	•	environment label such as:
Soft Indoor Light, High-Glare Study Space, Bright Transit, Night Exposure
	•	a short explanation of the current contributors such as:
“Overhead glare and sustained screen exposure are raising your visual load.”
	•	a primary CTA button: Soften Now
	•	small summary cards for:
today’s Light Burden Index
current comfort level
time since last recovery
	•	a preview of the daily timeline lower on the screen

The home screen should feel clear, elegant, and not overcrowded.

3. Daily Light Burden Timeline

Create a visually original full-screen daily timeline showing how visual burden accumulates over time. This should not be a standard bar or line graph. Use a glowing ribbon or flowing light wave that changes thickness, intensity, smoothness, and brightness across the day.

Show:
	•	morning, afternoon, evening, night
	•	strain spikes
	•	sustained high-burden periods
	•	recovery dips
	•	nighttime buildup

Include tapable event markers such as:
	•	8:40 AM transit glare spike
	•	1:10 PM prolonged laptop strain
	•	10:50 PM late-night exposure buildup
	•	3:30 PM recovery dip

When tapped, each event should reveal a small detail card showing:
	•	likely cause
	•	type of strain
	•	why burden rose
	•	what could have helped

This screen should be one of the most visually compelling screens in the app.

4. Environment Scan

Design a room or surroundings scan screen that helps users identify visually stressful conditions in their environment. This can feel like a camera-based or stylized scan interface.

Include:
	•	camera or environmental scan preview
	•	subtle overlay markers showing glare hotspots, bright zones, or harsh contrast areas
	•	labels such as:
glare-heavy
stable brightness
high contrast zone
softer viewing angle
	•	a plain-language recommendation panel, such as:
“This space is contributing more glare than brightness.”
“Move one seat away from the window reflection.”
“Lowering your screen angle may reduce strain.”

This screen should feel futuristic but believable, with clean overlays and a calm aesthetic.

5. Comfort Mode

Create a focused, minimal screen for applying a softer visual environment through the device.

Include controls for:
	•	brightness reduction
	•	warmer tone
	•	softer contrast
	•	reading comfort
	•	low stimulation mode

The main CTA can still be Soften Now, with the screen showing how the visual system becomes calmer once enabled.

Use soft toggles, sliders, and a visual preview of the before and after state. This screen should satisfy the challenge requirement that the app can not only track and quantify a sensory input but also influence or manipulate that same input.

6. Recovery Screen

Design a short visual recovery prompt screen for when burden becomes too high. This should feel quiet, grounded, and restorative.

Include:
	•	a short guided prompt like “Look away for 20 seconds”
	•	a calming visual progress ring or soft dimming animation
	•	a message like:
“Your visual load is high. A short reset can help reduce strain.”
	•	a subtle indication that the burden is dropping

Avoid meditation app clichés. Keep it elegant and practical.

7. Insights Screen

Create a clean insights screen with only 3 or 4 cards max. These insights should explain patterns without becoming chart-heavy or overwhelming.

Example cards:
	•	“Most of your strain happens during long study blocks.”
	•	“Night use is increasing your recovery time.”
	•	“Glare affects your comfort more than brightness.”
	•	“Short recovery breaks help, but not after extended evening exposure.”

Each card should feel smart, digestible, and useful. Add subtle trend visuals where appropriate, but keep it minimal.

8. Privacy & Controls

Design a final settings/privacy screen showing that the app is thoughtful and responsible.

Include:
	•	pause sensing
	•	private mode
	•	delete today’s data
	•	comfort sensitivity
	•	manual overrides
	•	explanation that the app is not for diagnosis
	•	note that data is not shared with employers, schools, or third parties

This screen should feel trustworthy and polished, not dull or purely technical.

Product and UX rules

Throughout the design, reinforce that Luma is:
	•	a sensory wellness app
	•	about cumulative visual strain and light burden
	•	about awareness before discomfort peaks
	•	designed to support comfort, better habits, and recovery

Do not make it feel like:
	•	a screen-time blocker
	•	a medical device
	•	a generic accessibility settings menu
	•	a normal health dashboard
	•	a basic brightness tool

Visual rules

Use:
	•	dark backgrounds
	•	subtle gradients and glow
	•	premium minimal typography
	•	rounded modules
	•	translucent panels
	•	sensory data visualizations that feel atmospheric and elegant
	•	soft color distinctions between states

Avoid:
	•	loud warning reds
	•	fitness-app charts everywhere
	•	bright neon cyberpunk overload
	•	excessive text density
	•	cluttered data-heavy layouts

The final result should feel like a competition-quality speculative mobile app concept that is original, believable, and visually memorable. It should clearly show how the product detects, visualizes, and influences an invisible sensory experience related to light and visual burden.
